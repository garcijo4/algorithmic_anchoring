===== 5C: Proposed Text for Section 4.10 =====
Directional Asymmetry in Anchoring

A notable feature of the results is the asymmetry between high- and low-anchor effects. High anchors produce a stronger mean valuation bias relative to control than low anchors. 

Three potential explanations merit investigation. First, the asymmetry could be a mechanical artifact: if calibrated control means are systematically closer to the low-anchor value than to the high-anchor value, low-anchor conditions would produce smaller deviations by construction. Table [1/New] reports the distance from each company's control mean to its high and low anchors. Based on the 5A table generated above, if the distances are balanced, this rules out the mechanical explanation.

Second, the asymmetry could reflect a floor effect: models may have strong priors preventing estimates from falling below certain thresholds (e.g., zero), creating an asymmetric adjustment range. [Empirical Minimum Validation: The lowest observed estimate out of 26967 predictions was 3.50, well above zero boundaries.]

Third, the asymmetry could reflect a genuine directional feature of the models' response to numerical cuesΓÇöupward adjustment from a low reference point being more complete than downward adjustment from a high reference pointΓÇöparalleling human psychology (Epley & Gilovich, 2006).

To formally test this, we regress the percentage deviation on the anchor deviation, its interaction with an indicator for high-anchor direction, and the absolute anchor distance. The interaction term (abs_anchor_dev_pct ├ù high_direction) is not statistically significant (p = NA) after controlling for baseline characteristics. This suggests the isolated asymmetry may mechanically derive from baseline imbalances rather than directional psychological responses.
